# Mistakes, Attempted Escapes, and a Spectacle on Southsea Common — The Tale of David Tyrie

It's late August, around about the same weekend the Victorious Festival takes place each year today, where seventy or eighty thousand people congregate on Southsea common for a weekend of festival frolicking and fun by the seaside.

Back then, in 1782, almost 250 years ago, it's said that a *hundred* thousand were gathered there, for a gathering of a different sort.

A hundred thousand people. The same as a sell out Victorious Festival at full capacity.

Out in the Solent, at Spithead, a fleet is assembled. It presents a spectacular view from Portsmouth, and a spectacular view from Ryde, too. At that time, the ships would only go into port if needed major repairs. Most of the time they'd be anchored at Spithead, then pop round to St Helens to wait for a fair wind. Right now, they were at Spithead, making ready for a relief operation down to Gibraltar, as well as escorting ships of the East India company on the first part of their much longer journey in search of tea.

A few days before, the Royal George, pride of Lord Howe's fleet, 100 guns on board, was taking on board supplies. A full complement of crew on board each ship, and the rest: friends, families and over familiar females. As they look towards Southsea Common, they hear a huge cheer go up. Twenty minutes later, an "oooh", and an "errrghh..." and a "oahhhh"...

A bit later again, there's some activity on the beach, a crowd surge perhaps, a bit like a pitch invasion or surge around a Formula One podium today. And then, a huddle of people on board ship as something very special is shown around.

So what just happened?

Back in those days, if you were a regular reader of the Hampshire Chronicle, you'd be well versed in reading about ship movements, the number of guns they had, where they'd supposedly been and where they were supposedly going, which Captains were on board, maybe even how many men. Common knowledge. But the sort of knowledge, in another form, that could also get you in trouble. A lot of trouble.

Six months before, David Tyrie, or Tie-ree, cocked up. A Scot, a native of Edinburgh, apprenticed in Leith, someone who could have made it as a successful businessman in London, if he hadn't got involved in a rather complicated fraud, Tyrie was now in the information business. Using spotters at the Naval Docks, and his own access to the Navy Office, where he'd previously worked, he was selling information about the state of the Navy to the French.

And now he was in Portsmouth. And everything was going wrong. The skipper he'd used before to take messages over to France under cover of being a smuggler was unavailable, so he'd had to take on another Captain. But as Tyrie was putting the latest information pack together, "Oh f--k....". That sinking feeling, in the pit of your stomach. "Oh crap"... For some time, Tyrie had been "borrowing" official lists from the Navy Office, copying them in his own hand, returning the original so no-one would know. "B*****ks". It was the original... he must have put the *copy*, the copy *in his own handwriting*, handwriting which *people in his previous office would know* to be his. "Crap, crap, crap."

It was too late now... he put the signals together, for getting into the harbour at the other side, sealed the papers for the French, and went to see his man.

Who wasn't overly comfortable with what he was being asked to do. Tyrie had told him when he first recruited him he was collating information for the Navy. That it would be officially checked by them before it was "passed on". But the young man hadn't been convinced, and had.. not quite made up the numbers and the names of the ships, but.. well, he massaged them a bit. If they were being checked anyway, they'd be corrected.

But now there was talk of sailing over to France, and a packet of signals  — what "signals"? Naval signals? This wasn't what he'd signed up for. So he went to take advice. Was asked where he'd got the papers. "We need to show these to someone."

Meanwhile, Tyrie, kicking himself about his mistake in putting his handwritten copied papers back, not the original, knew there could be trouble. "What am I gonna do? They're going to know? They're going to come after me?" He'd kept copies of the copies, of course he had, proof of information sent, but now they could be a liability?" "Well, I could take them, up to London, give them to someone there, someone no-one suspect", and so it was, his wife headed up to town on the very next coach. Took the papers to an old schoolmistress friend of hers: "can you take care of these for me, for my husband? But don"t let anyone see them? Please, he's in terrible trouble, and, please, can you do that?" The woman said yes, but she was suspicious. Looked at the papers. Took them to a friend. "Where did you get these? These are treasonous... We must show them to someone..."

In the Navy Office, a clerk comes across a document. The mice have been very active lately, papers going missing for a day or two and then magically reappearing. This was one such document. But it wasn't the original. It was a copy. And that handwriting was familiar.

A watch was placed on the school mistress's house. When Tyrie's wife reappeared a few days later, she was picked up.

A few days later, her husband was arrested.

They were held for questioning in London. Mrs Tyrie, just a messenger, was released, while Tyrie was sent to Newgate.

At that time, people went in and out of the Bridewell all the time. Prisons then were a form of enforced budget hotel accommodation, with everything sold as an add on. Visitors were frequent, bringing food and other niceties into the jail. This also gave an opportunity for networking. I'm not sure how it happened, but happen it did, and one of the visitors, a forger by trade, got friendly with Tyrie. Tyrie asked for his help: one of the friendlier guards had told him he was to be moved to Winchester for trial later that week. When he went, it would be early, 5 in the morning. But Tyrie had a plan. "We need a man on the outside, someone who can recognise me when they transfer me. If they wait at the Hyde Park Turnpike, they'd see me pass by, pass the word on. Get three or four men, dressed as waggoners, handkerchiefs over their faces, out on Bagshot Heath — everyone'll reckon them as smugglers and leave them alone. Tell them I'm a smuggler, that they're rescuing a smuggler. And get them to bring snuff. Lots of snuff. I know it sounds strange, but we can throw it in the guard's eyes. And a punch, for knocking the chains off."

As plans go, it sounds like it was a good one, like a scene in a film I remember from my childhood.  But it was perhaps a bit too rich for the forger and no rescue attempt was actually made. And so, Tyrie ended up in Winchester, on a charge of High Treason.

He made a motion to put off the trial: "My Lord, four of my witnesses aren't ready; a female witness, who is in Tothill-fields bridewell; two sailors, they're abroad; and another man, who lives in Ostend." "What are you talking about, not ready? You've had months to prepare: motion denied."

The case was swift. On being found guilty, he was asked, in the usual way, why sentence of death should not be passed. His response? "There's nothing I can say; I'm convicted because I'm poor, because I hadn't the means to bring my witnesses; this is all a mockery."

The judge donned his black cap and the most dreadful of sentences was passed.

But despite the grim fate awaiting him, Tyrie wasn't overly concerned. He was already working on another plan. For the previous week or two, he'd been busy, along with other prisoners in the cell. The walls had recently been whitewashed, but if you knew just where to look... there, just there... an irregular shaped line of cement; chip it out, ease out the plank, not plaster, and: a possible way out. At first glance it looked like brickwork, but the bricks were loose and could be easily taken out; and there were tools hidden in the gap there, too: a hammer; some chisels; a hand saw.

The execution date may have been approaching, but they must nearly be through the wall by now. It really couldn't be much further. "So how thick are the walls, then?" Tyre had asked one of the jailers. Big mistake. "Three feet? Why? Are you thinking of breaking out through it?" "No, not at all, not at all," sad Tyrie, "I was just... *curious*. But it does seem rather excessive."

But the jailer's curiosity had been piqued; he looked round the cell, tapped the wall every so often, here, there, there, here, and: tapped on wood, not stone; saw the jagged of what look like fresh cement. Broke through the panel, and: "Hah, so, not trying to escape then?"

A large crowd had gathered outside the jail by four that the morning, some of the keenest perhaps getting there the night before, sleeping out to guarantee a good view. By five, Tyrie was put into a coach, pulled by six horses, and set off for Portsmouth. "I was supposed to have been rescued, you know, the Smugglers offered do it, it's just they just wanted too much, way more than I could raise."

Like John the Painter before him, warning of how easy it would be to attack the Naval dock at Plymouth, Tyrie also had a message for the authorities: there was someone else out there, someone still giving information to the French.

Unlike John the Painter, there wouldn't be a flurry of official confessions, no execution day merch. Tyrie made it clear he had nothing to say, no final confession exclusives.

The crowd already gathered at Southsea Common was immense. A hundred thousand people, it's said. And out in the Solent, at Spithead, it was busier than normal there, too, as the expeditionary fleet took on stores, and crowds gathered on deck to look out at the spectacle on the common.

Tyrie was hanged for exactly twenty-two minutes; still not quite dead, he was lowered on to the stage; his privities cut off (ooohhh...), his bowels cut out (errrghh...), and his heart taken out (oahhhh). "Behold the heart of a traitor" and into a fire. Then his head was cut off, and his body quartered, and placed into a coffin, which was then buried under the pebbles on the beach. It is said, that as soon as the officers retired, sailors dug up the coffin, took out the body, and cut it in a thousand pieces, each one carrying away a piece of Tyrie's body to show to their messmates on board.

Whether any of the men on board the Royal George had acquired any of the body parts of the the traitor Tyrie as some some of macabre luck charm, I don't know. But I do know that three days after the execution, stores were still being loaded on board her.

During her last voyage, she'd been taking on more water than usual, so a running repair was also required. It was something and nothing, a small pipe which let water into the hold for cleaning the ship. But it needed replacing. It was usually about three feet below the surface of the water, a faff to get to, so they'd have to heel the ship — lean her over to the other side, to raise the pipe out of the water.

The guns on the port side of the ship were run out of the port-holes as far as they'd go, thae starboard ones pulled into the middle of the ship. She heeled over to one side, just enough to provide access to the pipe that needed replacing. Each wave brought some water into through the port holes on the lower side, as usually happened, and the men had great fun chasing the mice that tried to escape it.

Stores were still being brought on board, not least the rum, so it made sense to do that on the lower side — easier to heave up the barrels. Several men went over to that side of the ship to help. The ship leant a tiny bit more. The barrels were brought on board. And the ship leaned a little more. The ship's carpenter sought out the officers in charge, told them "she's heeling too much." The first ignored him, the second gave him a bollocking: "if you you think you can manage the ship better than I can, you'd better take the command". A slight breeze sprang up, and she leaned further. The cannon-balls started sliding; the *cannons* started sliding. And in just a moment more, the Royal George tipped onto her side, and went down.

The watch on deck was 200 men or so. There were hundreds more, six, seven, eight hundred maybe, below deck; men, women — wives, and not — children, merchants, traders, and more. The men on deck could climb up the rigging — the ship was only in shallow water, the top of the masts were visible above the waterline after she sank — but the rest were lost. Two hundred so were saved, who knows how many were lost.

Bodies were washed ashore at Ryde and Southsea, and further along the coast. But there was also one very unusual survivor, at least. On board the ship, kept on the main deck, was a certain amount of livestock — fresh milk and meat for the voyage, presumably. Amongst which were two sheep, who managed to make it off the ship and swam to Ryde. Between them, an arm round each one, a young child. A young child aged between two and three years old. They'd been on board with their parents, ow lost, but somehow, somehow. Saved by the sheep.

If you're ever in Ryde, there's still a memorial there, to the hundreds who died on that fateful day. It's Royal George Memorial Gardens in Ryde, just off the roundabout, by the Castle Hotel.

There was no way the ship could be raised at the time, even though she was in shallow water. The size of her, the water inside her, the water above her, was just too much. As it was, the Royal George remained a hazard for many years, only to be blasted to smithereens in the 1840s to clear the obstacle she presented.

As for David Tyrie, who knows whether there were stories told of what happened to his remains that day, of how his finger bones were used to stopper glass bottles, perhaps, maybe how his hands — the hands of a hanged, and quartered, man — were used to make a particularly effective hand of glory — do you know about the hand of glory? That's maybe a tale for another day. But what I do know is, what happened to his head: because the keeper of Gosport Bridewell got hold of it. And put it on show for a shilling a go. And one day, a sailor, lately arrived from the West-Indies, took his girlfriend to see it — well, you would, wouldn't you? — took her for a nice glass of wine, took her to the Bridewell, had a quiet word with the gaoler — "can we see the head?" And the gaoler disappeared for a few minutes, returned with what looked like a tray, with something on it, covered with a cloth. Carefully, lifted off the cloth.... A shriek, and immediately, the young woman fainted. She was taken home, and put to bed. But he never rose again. And the sailor, distraught at what he'd done, then attempted to take his own life.

So such is the tale, or tales, of the sinking of the Royal George, and a few days before it, of the execution of David Tyrie.
