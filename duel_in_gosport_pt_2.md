SPRING ASSIZES. WESTERN CIRCUIT.-Winchester, March 2.

...

There is a report prevalent in the town that Lieutenant Hawkey, who, our readers may remember, was one of the principals in the unhappy duel which took place some months since, near Southampton; the result of which was the death of Captain Seton, will surrender to take his trial at the present assizes; but we believe the fact to be that Lieutenant Pym, who was Mr. Hawkey's second at the duel, will surrender and be tried, but that Mr. Hawkey will not do so at present. We understand that Mr. Cockburn and Mr. Sergeant Kinglake are retained for the defence. We need scarcely say that the above report has caused considerable excitement, as the parties are all so well known in the neighbourhood. Nothing of importance has taken place in the Crown Court to-day.

THE FATAL DUEL AT GOSPORT.

The following sketch of the conclusion of the trial of Lieutenant Hawkey, R.M., appeared in all but a small part of our impression yesterday:—

Mr. Cockburn having concluded a mast eloquent address, called Colonel Swaile, Colonel Gordon, Colonel Stephens, and a great many officers of the marine corps and other gentlemen, who gave the prisoner the highest character as an officer and a gentleman, and as a person of a very peaceable and kind disposition.

Mr Baron Piatt then proceeded to sum up the case to the Jury, and laid the law down to them to be, that if two persons go out to fight a duel after a previous quarrel, and one kill the other, all persons, whether principals or seconds, are guilty of murder. If, therefore, they thought that the evidence in this case made out to their satisfaction that Mr. Hawkey had shot Mr. Seton in a duel, it would be their bounden duty to find him guilty on the present indictment. He then went minutely through the evidence, commenting upon it as he went on: and concluded by telling them, that whatever they might think the provocation had been to the prisoner by the conduct of Mr. Seton to his (the prisoner's) wife, and however they might commiserate the wounded feelings of the husband, yet they must not allow that feeling to prevent them doing their duty, and if therefore, they thought the charge wm made out in evidence against the prisoner, they were bound to convict him.

At the conclusion of the learned baron's address, the jury without the hesitation of a single instant— almost without the formality of consulting together—returned a verdict of *Not Guilty*.

The announcement was received with a violent burst of approbation, which the officers of the Court strove in vain to repress. The Learned Judge commanded silence, and was compelled to order one of the most prominent and noisy of the crowd into custody. The party, however, managed to abscond; for nobody seemed disposed to make very violent efforts for his apprehension.

Lieutenant Hawkey, immediately on his liberation, was surrounded by friends and brother officers, offering him their hearty congratulations; and upon his exit from the Court he was loudly cheered by the people.

Mr. Baron Platt was also saluted with hearty cheers.
